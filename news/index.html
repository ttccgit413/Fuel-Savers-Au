Fuel Crisis

That $7.10 petrol photo everyone shared is fake. Here’s what’s actually happening to your bill.

The viral image from North Melbourne was a display glitch, not a real price. But the experts who confirmed that are more worried about what comes next.
SR
Sarah Rennick, Consumer Affairs
Thursday 3 April 2026, 8:14am AEST

When a photo of a 7-Eleven Mobil price board showing $7.10 per litre appeared on Reddit last week, it spread faster than almost anything else during this crisis. Screenshots circulated on WhatsApp, Facebook groups, and neighbourhood apps within hours. A federal MP posted it. Radio stations read it out on air.

The price was never real.

✓ Fact check The $7.10 figure was a known display glitch on certain 7-Eleven Mobil LED price boards. When prices change rapidly, some boards temporarily show corrupted numbers before resetting. The station’s actual price at the time was $2.41 per litre for regular unleaded. 7-Eleven confirmed the fault. The sign has since been fixed.

So that is the good news. Nobody paid $7.10 per litre in North Melbourne.

The problem is that the energy analysts who spent ten minutes confirming it was a glitch have been spending the rest of their week quietly alarmed about something the viral photo accidentally got right: the direction prices are heading, and how little buffer Australia actually has.

What Albanese said in Wednesday night’s national address

In a rare prime-time broadcast, Prime Minister Anthony Albanese addressed the nation about the fuel crisis. He was measured. He urged Australians not to panic buy. He announced a further cut to the fuel excise, adding 5.7 cents per litre on top of Monday’s 26.3 cent reduction, a combined package worth $2.55 billion.

“The war in the Middle East has caused the biggest spike in petrol and diesel prices in history. Australia is not an active participant in this war. But all Australians are paying higher prices because of it. The economic shocks caused by this war will be with us for months.” PM Anthony Albanese — national address, 1 April 2026

What he did not say plainly is that Australia has approximately 28 days of national fuel reserve remaining, and that figure assumes supply shipments keep arriving on schedule. The International Energy Agency requires member nations to hold 90 days. Australia has been non-compliant since 2012.

Western Australia invoked emergency powers. “We don’t know where the fuel is.”

Within hours of the Prime Minister’s address, Western Australian Premier Roger Cook announced his state was activating emergency powers under the Fuel, Energy and Power Resources Act 1972. The trigger was fuel companies refusing to share their own supply data with the government.

Only three of six fuel companies had responded to a government request for information on stock levels, shipment routes, and delivery schedules.

“In some cases, we don’t know where the fuel is.” WA Premier Roger Cook — 1 April 2026

That sentence received less coverage than the $7.10 display glitch.

The excise cut: $19 off a tank sounds good. Here’s why it’s not enough.

Monday’s announcement halved the fuel excise to 26 cents per litre for three months. The NRMA’s Peter Khoury said flatly that a similar cut after Russia’s invasion of Ukraine in 2022 “just didn’t register at the bowser.” Oil companies have a documented history of absorbing excise reductions rather than passing them on in full. The ACCC is monitoring. But monitoring is not guaranteeing.

And the cut expires 30 June. If the Hormuz situation has not resolved by then, prices could spike by 26 to 32 cents per litre in a single day with no government buffer remaining.

What actually happens next

Now Hormuz remains effectively closed to commercial tankers. Asian refineries providing 80% of Australia’s refined fuel are drawing down their own crude reserves while seeking alternative routes.
Mid‑April Energy Minister Bowen says supply is “secure until mid-April.” Six expected shipments have already been deferred. Nine additional cargoes arranged. The buffer is thin.
May+ Ampol’s CEO this week: “If this runs longer than a few weeks, you’re going to see pressure on prices and global supply chains.” It has already run longer than a few weeks.
30 June The excise cut expires. Without resolution, prices could spike 26 to 32 cents per litre in a single day with no government measure remaining to cushion it.

The Australians who are not panicking

While petrol station queues stretched around the block and social media shared a display glitch as evidence of collapse, a smaller group of Australian drivers had quietly changed their behaviour. No jerry cans. Just a set of specific habits and tools that cut their monthly fuel bill by $130 to $180 without changing their lifestyle.

Most of it is information asymmetry. The average driver pulls into the nearest servo out of habit. The informed driver checks Petrol Spy first and drives 2km further to a station 28 cents per litre cheaper. On a 60-litre tank that is $16.80 saved per fill. Two fills a month: $33.60 from one free app that takes 30 seconds to open.

That is before supermarket docket stacking, the gift card double-dip through NRMA (14 cents per litre off in a single transaction), Victoria’s new mandatory 2pm price predictor, and for anyone with an ABN, fleet fuel cards currently running 8 to 12 cents below pump price throughout the crisis.

A note on the photo that started this

The $7.10 image spread because it confirmed what people already feared. That fear is not irrational. Prices have risen 50 cents per litre in six weeks. Western Australia just invoked emergency powers because the government does not know where some of the fuel is. The Prime Minister gave a rare national broadcast about it.

The sign was a glitch. The crisis is not.
